ThreadSense for Facebook — User Guide

Version 0.1.0

ThreadSense is a Chrome extension that analyzes posts and comments you can already see on Facebook desktop and shows local heuristic risk estimates for troll-like behavior and fake-account patterns. It runs entirely in your browser and does not send data to any server.

Important: ThreadSense never claims anyone is a troll, bot, or fake account. Scores are pattern-based guesses and can be wrong. Use them as one input among many—not as proof.

1. What ThreadSense does

While you browse Facebook (feed, posts, groups, etc.), ThreadSense:

  • Finds visible posts and comments in the page.
  • Extracts comment/post text and optional public profile cues.
  • Scores each item with two independent heuristics:
    • Troll-behavior risk — hostile, baiting, repetitive, or inflammatory patterns.
    • Fake-account risk — scam language, link spam, default avatars, thin profiles, etc.
  • Annotates content with badges you can click for explanations.

Analysis is reactive: as new comments appear while you scroll, ThreadSense re-runs on visible content. There is no background scraping of other pages.

What ThreadSense does not do

  • Hide, report, block, or reply to comments automatically.
  • Read private messages or anything you cannot already see on the page.
  • Store names, profile URLs, or comment text after you close the tab.
  • Judge political affiliation, protected traits, or mental health.

2. Quick start

  1. Install ThreadSense from the Chrome Web Store or the tester link.
  2. In Chrome, open chrome://extensions and make sure ThreadSense is enabled.
  3. Go to facebook.com.
  4. Scroll until you see posts with comments.
  5. Look for ThreadSense badges next to comments and some posts.
  6. Click a badge to open the explanation popover.

If you see no badges at all, see Troubleshooting.

3. Badges, highlights, and popovers

Badge types

  • Risk badge — shows enabled risk types and levels, e.g. Fake: Med (45) · Troll: High (72).
  • No signals badge — gray No signals when the “Tag every comment” setting is on and nothing triggered.

Score levels

Each comment/post can have:

Level Range Meaning
Low 0–39 Weak pattern; often harmless or ambiguous.
Medium 40–69 Stronger pattern; worth a closer look.
High 70–100 Many signals combined; be cautious and double-check.

The badge color and any left-border highlight use the higher of the two scores (troll or fake).

Popover contents

Click a badge to see:

  • Troll and fake scores, each with Low/Medium/High labels.
  • Short bullet reasons (for example “hostile phrases”, “many links”, “default avatar + numeric URL”).
  • A disclaimer reminding you that these are heuristic estimates, not verdicts.

4. Toolbar popup

Click the ThreadSense icon in the Chrome toolbar to open the popup.

Tab stats

Stat Meaning
Scanned Number of posts/comments analyzed on the active Facebook tab.
High / Medium / Low How many items fell into each combined level band.

The popup shows “—” if the active tab is not a Facebook page with the extension running.

Quick settings

In the popup, you can toggle:

  • Enable on Facebook – turn ThreadSense on/off for Facebook.
  • Show low signals – show or hide Low-level badges.
  • Tag every comment – give every comment a badge (including “No signals”).

Changes apply immediately to the current Facebook tab.

5. Settings (options page)

You can open the full settings page by:

  • Clicking All settings in the toolbar popup, or
  • Going to Chrome’s extension list → ThreadSense → Extension options.

Key options

  • Enable extension – master on/off switch for analysis.
  • Show low-signal results – choose whether to show Low-level badges.
  • Tag every comment – every analyzable comment gets a badge.
  • Sensitivity – Low / Normal / High; higher sensitivity will flag more items but may increase false positives.
  • Fake-account risk – turn fake-pattern scoring on/off.
  • Troll-behavior risk – turn troll-pattern scoring on/off.
  • Public profile context – optionally use visible profile cues (like default avatar or thin profiles) to slightly adjust fake risk. Data stays local and is not stored between sessions.

You can reset to default settings at any time from the options page.

6. Risk criteria and thresholds

ThreadSense uses a set of heuristic signals to estimate troll and fake-account risk. This section explains what it looks for and roughly when a signal is considered active.

6.0 Phrase lists (where the “database” comes from)

ThreadSense does not look up words on the internet or use a separate online database. The insults, bait lines, and scam phrases it checks for are built into the extension and update when you install a newer version. If a comment trips a warning, the popover can show you the actual words it noticed—in quotes—under that reason.

List Purpose Main sources
HOSTILE_TERMS Insults, slurs, dismissive/hostile language Curated English terms; profanity/slurs informed by dariusk/wordfilter and LDNOOBW; Tagalog profanity from filipino-badwords-list (MIT); extended PH insults cross-referenced with hate-speech research (Cabasag 2019, Cruz 2020); PH political discourse terms cross-referenced with Ong & Cabañes (2018).
BAIT_PHRASES Debate-bait, challenges, bad-faith questions Curated English + Tagalog/PH patterns observed on Facebook comments.
SCAM_PHRASES Scam, promo, DM/GCash, work-from-home spam Curated English + Tagalog patterns common in comment scams.

Word lists are refreshed when you install a newer version of the extension. The checker is a best-effort tool: it can flag something by mistake, miss a nasty comment that uses unusual wording, or overlook insults phrased in a way that is not on the list yet.

6.1 Troll‑behavior risk

Goal: approximate “Does this text and behavior on this page look hostile, baiting, or repetitive?”

  • Hostile / insulting phrases
    Looks for clear insults or slurs (including some Tagalog/PH political insults). One strong term, or several weaker ones in the same comment, can push this signal up. Rough weight: up to ~30 points.
  • Bait / antagonistic phrases
    Challenge phrases and bait cues (e.g. “prove it”, “debate me”, etc.). One strong bait phrase or several lighter ones can activate it. Rough weight: up to ~15 points.
  • Excessive ALL CAPS
    Treated as shouting when:
    • Text length is at least around 25 characters, and
    • A large majority (≈65%+) of letters or words are uppercase.
    Rough weight: up to ~15 points.
  • Repeated punctuation
    Emphasis patterns like !!!, ???, ?!?! with at least 3 repeated characters. Rough weight: up to ~10 points.
  • Near‑duplicate comments
    Same author posting essentially the same text multiple times on one page (copy‑paste spam or repeated replies). Needs at least 2 similar comments by the same author. Rough weight: up to ~20 points.
  • Author hostility frequency
    Same author posting multiple hostile comments in the same thread/page. Kicks in when they have several visible comments and about half or more are hostile. Rough weight: up to ~15 points.
  • Condescension density
    Several condescending phrases or a mix of condescension plus other hostile markers in one comment. Rough weight: up to ~15 points.
  • Short + hostile
    Very short comments (around 3–8 words) that are mostly an insult or attack. Rough weight: up to ~10 points.
  • Minimum content rule
    A comment needs at least about 10 characters and 2 words before troll scoring runs. Very short/unreadable content is skipped (may still get a neutral badge if you tag every comment).

6.2 Fake‑account risk

Goal: approximate “Does this account + comment look like scam/spam/farm patterns?”

  • Scam / promotional language
    Phrases used in scams or promos (e.g., “dm me for help”, “earn daily”, etc., tuned for your audience). One or more strong phrases can push this signal high. Rough weight: up to ~35 points.
  • Link‑heavy comments
    Comments that mainly push links (one or more URLs with high link density). Usually not applied to the original post author. Rough weight: up to ~20 points.
  • Repeated link sharing
    Same author posting link‑heavy comments at least twice on one page. Rough weight: up to ~20 points.
  • Suspicious name patterns
    Display names that look auto‑generated or ID‑like (all digits, long digit suffixes, odd letter‑digit mixes). Rough weight: ~6–20 points depending on strength.
  • Numeric‑only profile URL
    Profile links like profile.php?id=… instead of a human vanity URL. Rough weight: up to ~12 points.
  • Default profile photo
    Account appears to use Facebook’s default gray silhouette avatar, based on avatar detection mode. Rough weight: up to ~24 points.
  • Default avatar + numeric URL combo
    When both a default-looking avatar and a numeric profile URL are detected, extra weight is added on top of each individual signal (around +8 points).
  • Burst commenting
    Same account posting many comments on the same page (around 5+ visible comments). Rough weight: up to ~15 points.
  • Generic / engagement‑farming content
    Very short, generic replies (emojis only, “nice”, “wow”, etc.), especially when combined with links or repetition. Rough weight: about 8–10 points.
  • Public profile context (weak, capped)
    Optional profile-based hints (default avatar, very low friend/follower counts, “joined recently” etc.) when you have profile context enabled. Combined profile signals can contribute up to around 35 points, but cannot by themselves push someone to “High” risk.

6.3 Avatar detection modes

  • Strict – fewer “default photo” flags but may miss some silhouettes; requires clearer evidence.
  • Aggressive – flags more suspected defaults but increases chance of false positives; inferred-only detections may be down‑weighted or ignored.

6.4 Combining scores

  • Both troll and fake scores are calculated from 0–100.
  • A sensitivity multiplier applies:
    • Low ≈ 0.75×
    • Normal ≈ 1.1×
    • High ≈ 1.35×
    then scores are clamped to 0–100.
  • Very small scores (below about 8) are suppressed to 0 to reduce noise.
  • The badge’s overall level uses the higher of (troll score, fake score).
  • Verified pages skip fake‑account scoring, and post authors may skip some fake patterns related to link repetition.

8. Troubleshooting

Problem Things to try
No badges at all Check that the extension is enabled in chrome://extensions. Make sure you are on facebook.com. Reload the Facebook tab (Ctrl+R / Cmd+R) after enabling.
Popup shows “—” stats The active tab must be a Facebook page with content loaded. Switch to a Facebook tab and wait a moment.
Too many things are flagged Lower sensitivity to Low, turn off “Show low signals”, or disable one of the risk types.
Too few things are flagged Raise sensitivity to High, enable both risk types, and enable public profile context.
Badges on the wrong element This can happen when Facebook changes its layout. If you see this often, report it with a screenshot and page type (feed, group, etc.).

9. Responsible use

  • Scores are approximate. Sarcasm, jokes, or quoted insults can be misread.
  • Real people can have default avatars or new accounts; “fake risk” is not proof.
  • Use ThreadSense to slow down, reconsider, and look more closely—not as a final judge of any person.
